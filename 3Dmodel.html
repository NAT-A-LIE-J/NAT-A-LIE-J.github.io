  • GESM120: Art and Architecture in the Home

3D Model Falling Water

In this creative project, I reimagine Frank Lloyd Wright's iconic Fallingwater as a site of urban tenement living to confront dominant cultural attitudes about class, wealth, and architectural romanticism in the United States.

By overlaying the cramped, overburdened conditions of early 20th-century tenement housing onto Falling water, I explore how home can be a site of resistance to class-based inequality. I introduce key elements of tenement life into the organic architecture and individualized luxury of Wright’s design. The resulting visual dissonance is intentional: a critique of how architectural “greatness” often masks the social realities it excludes. I resist the ideal of the American home as a private, privileged space disconnected from struggle. Instead, my reimagined Fallingwater foregrounds the resilience of immigrant and working-class families whose experiences of “home” are marked not by harmony, but by hardship, improvisation, and shared survival.

I chose a 3D model format because it allows viewers to actively engage with and visually experience the contradictions in these spaces from multiple perspectives. Inspired by Jacob Riis's documentation in "How the Other Half Lives" and Dolores Hayden’s analysis of urban landscapes in "The Power of Place," this project demonstrates how architecture can simultaneously uphold cultural narratives of prosperity and reveal systemic inequalities. Ultimately, this reimagined Fallingwater serves as a critique of romanticized architectural grandeur, making visible those who have historically been neglected.

Dumbbell Tenements Encircle Fallingwater

Once a sanctuary in nature, Fallingwater is now boxed in by dumbbell tenements—an infamous form of housing meant to improve living conditions, but which instead intensified overcrowding. They block views of the surrounding natural environment, a key appeal of Falling Water’s identity, to reflect tenement living’s typical isolation from natural landscapes. These narrow, air-shafted buildings press in on all sides, symbolizing how reform efforts often fall short when structural inequality remains.

Train Through the Guest and Main House

A train line slices through Fallingwater, severing access from the guest and main house. In tenement-style living, such infrastructure is more than inconvenience—it fractures families and communities. Here, the intrusion mimics how poor urban communities were cut through by railways and highways, disrupting privacy, unity, and peace, often with little power to stop this construction.

Labor on Display

Clotheslines stretch across balconies, logs, and beams—visual markers of daily, traditionally female, labor. Alongside this, cramped outdoor areas double as workspaces, where residents sew, mend, or assemble goods for piecework. These signs of labor blur the line between home and workplace, exposing the economic struggles and exploitation faced by working-class families. Here, leisure gives way to labor.

Neglect Made Visible

Piles of dirt accumulate near outhouses and rusted shared sinks, while barred windows and crumbling surfaces demonstrate sustained neglect. These conditions reflect how tenement buildings—unlike elite architecture—were rarely maintained. Here, Fallingwater’s pristine modernism decays into a visual record of disinvestment.

Curtains for Walls, Beds for Borders

Open spaces collapse into tight quarters, hastily divided by curtains and makeshift partitions. Shared beds and sparse furnishings dominate the rooms, replacing privacy with proximity. In this altered Fallingwater, the flowing floor plan is obstructed—mirroring the density and improvisation of tenement life. Communal courtyards and gardens speak to both the hardship of overcrowding and the solidarity formed in shared struggle.
